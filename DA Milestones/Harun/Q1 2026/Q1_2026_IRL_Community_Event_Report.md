## Key Themes from the Fireside Discussion

### Summit Turnout and Community Maturity

Despite a difficult market environment, CATS was held at capacity. The strong turnout, made up largely of young people, signalled that Cardano's appeal in Africa is grounded in fundamentals: people came to build, not to speculate.

### Stablecoin Adoption at Africa Tech Summit

At the Africa Tech Summit (11-12 February), stablecoins and cross-border payments dominated the conversation across nearly every exhibitor and session. The group reflected that peer-to-peer stablecoin transactions are already a present reality in African markets, particularly in Nigeria. This is not a future aspiration but an existing behaviour that blockchain infrastructure needs to support and build upon.

### Diversity and Inclusion Beyond Developers

CATS drew a notably diverse audience. Alongside developers, the summit included lawyers discussing intellectual property and crypto regulation, designers building user-facing products, marketers, and government representatives. The cohort agreed this breadth of representation was a positive and necessary sign for the long-term health of the ecosystem.

### Africans Solving African Problems

A breakout session facilitated by Adam Dean, Sam Leathers, and Kyle asked participants what problems they would want to solve as Africans using Cardano. The framing was deliberate: external participants were there to listen, not prescribe. African builders proposed their own solutions while Cardano Foundation representatives took notes. The cohort reflected that this felt like genuine representation, with the broader Cardano community playing a supporting role rather than a directing one.

### Education as the Primary Barrier to Entry

Across summit sessions, one-on-one conversations, and the masterclass led by Dan Baruka, the most consistent finding was that developers want to build on Cardano but do not know where to start. The DevEx Working Group sessions, developer onboarding calls, and the Cardano Developer Pathway were each identified as direct responses to this gap. The cohort also noted that discoverability remains a challenge: many developers who would benefit from these resources are simply unaware they exist.

### The Distribution Gap

A recurring theme surfaced through a conversation about a graveyard of projects across different blockchain ecosystems: building a product is not sufficient on its own. Distribution, getting a finished product into the hands of the people who need it, is an underaddressed problem in the ecosystem. The cohort discussed community-building, consistent communication, and the Cardano Hubs model as partial solutions, while acknowledging that a more deliberate ecosystem-wide strategy is still needed.

### Cardano Hubs as a Scalable Model

The group highlighted the Cardano Hubs model as the most promising path to sustainable grassroots reach. Replicating the Blockchain Center Nairobi model across other African cities would bring locally relevant education directly to developers and communities. The cohort called for more hubs, more frequent regional events, and for CATS itself to be replicated across the continent.
