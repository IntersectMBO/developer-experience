# Q1 2026: Community Engagement

**By Harun Waweru Mwangi**

Q1 2026 was defined by connections, across countries, communities, and conversations. From a landmark Cardano summit in Nairobi to one-on-one sessions with developers finding their footing in the ecosystem, the quarter reinforced what this work is really about: meeting people where they are and helping them take the next step.

Here is a look at what I have been up to.

---

### The Blockchain Center Nairobi

The Blockchain Center continues to be a reliable home base for Cardano community activity in Nairobi.

#### Cardano Study Session (January 15, 2026)

I attended a Cardano study session at the Blockchain Center in the role of mentor. As in the previous quarter, these sessions bring together developers at various stages of their learning journey and provide a space for peer learning and hands-on guidance. Showing up in a mentorship capacity matters here, as many of the developers working through the material benefit as much from having someone to talk to as from the content itself.

#### Cardano Hub Nairobi, Monthly Meetup (January 29, 2026)

The January monthly meetup drew a solid turnout of attendees keen to learn about Cardano. It continues to serve as the most reliable recurring touchpoint for local community engagement, welcoming both newcomers and established community members in an accessible, informal setting.

---

### Nairobi's February Tech Week

February brought a remarkable concentration of events to Nairobi that made the week feel like a turning point for Cardano's presence in East Africa.

#### Africa Tech Summit (February 10-11, 2026)

Africa Tech Summit drew builders, investors, and innovators from across the continent. Cardano was well represented, with a dedicated corner shared by the Cardano Foundation, Intersect, and other ecosystem organisations. The standout theme of the conference was stablecoins. Cross-border payments and peer-to-peer stablecoin transactions were everywhere, reflecting how deeply digital financial tools have already taken root in African markets. The conversations were energetic and grounded; this was not an audience speculating about the future, but one that is already living in it.

#### Sunset and Stablecoins, Anzens, BlockAi and Encryptus (February 11, 2026)

On the evening of the second day, I attended a networking event hosted by Anzens, BlockAi, and Encryptus. Shantnoo, Acting CEO of Anzens, gave an inspiring address on mobile money and stablecoins, a timely reminder that the most meaningful blockchain adoption in Africa is happening not through hype, but through practical financial tools that solve real, daily problems. The room was full of people who understand this at a gut level, and the conversations reflected that clarity.

#### Cardano Africa Tech Summit (CATS) (February 13, 2026)

CATS was the highlight of the quarter. Sponsored by WADA and the Blockchain Center Nairobi, the summit drew a crowd that defied a difficult market moment as the venue was at capacity. Seeing young people, builders, designers, lawyers, and community organisers all in the same tent was a reminder that Cardano's appeal in Africa is not narrowly technical.

It also marked the first time all four members of the Intersect Developer Advocate cohort met in person. Representing Intersect alongside Dan Baruka, Emmanuel (Uche), and Amar Titi, we were able to present a united front, share what the Developer Experience program is doing, and listen to what builders on the ground are asking for.

Dan led a developer deep-dive masterclass at the summit, walking a room of eager attendees through a pathway into Cardano development. The consistent finding was that people want to build, they are not intimidated by the technology, but they do not know where to start. That is exactly the gap the Developer Advocate program exists to close.

A breakout session facilitated by Adam Dean, Sam Leathers, and Kyle asked a powerful question: what problems would you want to solve as an African using Cardano? The room filled with answers. The sentiment was clear. African builders want to define their own problems and their own solutions. The role of the broader Cardano community is to provide the tools, the support, and the funding pathways. That message landed loudly, and the Cardano Foundation representatives in the room were listening closely.

One conversation that stuck with me came from a developer who pointed out a graveyard of projects from previous hackathons across different blockchains. His question was: what is Cardano doing differently? The honest answer is that we are still working it out, but the fact that Intersect's programs, the Developer Advocate role, and WADA's community infrastructure all exist suggests we are at least asking the right questions.

### One-on-One Outreach

Alongside the events, much of the quarter's community work happened in individual conversations.

Vinit Inamke and Vinay Devabhakthuni of Konma Lab attended a DevEx Working Group session to share the work they are building: HaskLedger, Carbon Ledger, and MyOS. The conversation was rich with ideas about how community builders in India and Africa are solving the developer retention problem, not just through education but through building real projects that create employment for the developers they train. Connecting them to Intersect's open source programs and committee structures was a natural next step.

Tony Kiriungi and Jayson Amati were both introduced to the Cardano ecosystem and the Maintainer Retainer Program. Both are at the starting line. They know they want to contribute, and the sessions gave them a clearer map of where to go. Each left with a concrete task: review active Cardano repositories and pick one to engage with.

Kahindi James, representing the County Students Association Presidents Kenya, an organisation with over 600,000 student members, came in following a meeting at the Cardano Africa Tech Summit. The conversation opened up a potential channel to a vast student population across Kenyan counties and introduced Intersect's community programs to a previously untouched audience.

Kadiri Ahmed Tunde reached out to understand how to navigate the Cardano ecosystem as a developer. He was particularly interested in Haskell, and we spent the session walking through the Haskell learning path alongside the Aiken smart contract path. He left with a clearer sense of where to start, which communities to join, and why being part of the Intersect Discord early would benefit him in the long run.

---

### Sentiment

The consistent thread across all of this quarter's interactions was readiness. People are ready to build, ready to learn, and ready to contribute. What they need is orientation. The barrier is not motivation, it is the first step. That is an encouraging place to be, and it gives the Developer Advocate work a clear, concrete purpose heading into Q2.
